A LOW-COST COLOR VISION SYSTEM FOR  
AUTOMATIC ESTIMATION OF APPLE FRUIT ORIENTATION  
AND MAXIMUM EQUATORIAL DIAMETER 
A. Mizushima,  R. Lu 
ABSTRACT. The overall objective of this research was to develop an in-field presorting and grading system to separate 
undersized and defective fruit from fresh market-grade apples. To achieve this goal, a cost-effective machine vision 
inspection prototype was built, which consisted of a low-cost color camera, LED lights, and a generic bi-cone conveyor. 
Algorithms were developed for accurate estimation of pixels per unit dimension from images acquired under the close-
range imaging configuration and for real-time estimation of apple orientation, shape, and maximum equatorial diameter. 
The machine vision system was tested and evaluated for ‘Delicious’ (D), ‘Empire’ (EM), ‘Golden Delicious’ (GD), and 
‘Jonagold’ (JG) apples at a speed of four fruit per second. The variable pixels per unit dimension method achieved 
superior results for area estimation, compared to the conventional image distortion correction and area estimation 
methods. The orientation estimation algorithm had 87.6% and 86.2% accuracies for D and GD apples, respectively, 
within ±20° of actual fruit orientation, but it performed less satisfactorily for round-shaped EM and JG apples. The 
machine vision system achieved good fruit maximum equatorial diameter estimations, with an overall root mean squared 
error of 1.79 mm for the four varieties of apple, and it had a two-size grading error of 4.3%, versus 15.1% by a 
mechanical sizing machine. The system provides a cost-effective means for sorting apples for size. 
Keywords. Apple, Grading, In-field, Machine vision, Sizing, Sorting. 
urrently, apples are still harvested manually using 
the ladder and picking bag. After the picking bag 
is filled with apples (weighing about 20 kg), the 
worker carries it to a bin and carefully empties 
the fruit into the bin, which can hold up to 400 to 500 kg of 
apples. In the U.S., harvest bin trailers, which can carry up 
to 5 to 6 bins, are now commonly used during the apple 
harvest. Recently, some growers have begun to use mobile 
orchard platforms and mechanical harvest aid machines to 
improve the harvest efficiency. A harvest platform allows 
workers to directly dump the harvested fruit from the 
picking bag into a bin, while a harvest aid machine is 
usually equipped with a conveyor and a bin filler. Two 
commercial companies in the U.S. recently developed 
vacuum-style harvest aid machines to assist apple harvest 
(Warner, 2009; Luo et al., 2012). These machines have no 
or limited in-field sorting capabilities. 
In current harvest practice, all harvested fruit of different 
quality grades are placed into the same fruit bins, and they 
are then transported to the packinghouse. After storage, the 
apples are graded, sorted, and packed. High-quality apples 
are sold to the retailer as fresh, while culls (i.e., inferior and 
defective) are sent to the fruit processor for making 
processed products (i.e., juice, applesauce, canning, fresh 
cut, etc.). This practice means that growers have to pay 
almost the same cost for storing, grading, and sorting culls as 
that for premium-quality fresh apples, even though the price 
for processing apples is only a fraction of that for fresh 
apples. Due to the high costs for packinghouse operations, 
growers may not break even if a high percentage of culls are 
present. Therefore, there is a need for in-field grading and 
sorting technology to remove inferior and/or defective apples 
in the orchard, so as to achieve savings in postharvest storage 
and packing, reduce postharvest disease and pest problems, 
and enhance inventory management (Mizushima and Lu, 
2011). However, in-field presorting will incur machinery cost 
and may also require additional labor cost for apple growers. 
Mizushima and Lu (2011) did an economic analysis by 
comparing the cost of presorting apples in the orchard with 
that of directly storing and packing all harvested apples in 
the packinghouse. They concluded that in-field presorting 
will be beneficial for the average apple grower (~21 ha) in 
the U.S. if the machinery cost is no more than $30,000 unit-1. 
They also provided guidelines for the development of in-
field presorting systems.

autonomous, self-propelled vehicle or on a harvest trailer

which include, but are not limited to, reception of fruit from 
pickers, sorting and grading of each apple (i.e., juice, 
processing, or fresh market), and delivery of the graded 
fruit into specific bins without causing damage. To sort and 
grade apples in the field, an appropriately designed 
machine vision system needs to be developed. 
In-line machine vision-based inspection systems are 
now widely used in packinghouses. These systems are 
designed for precision sorting of apples into as many 
quality grades as customers desire at a high speed (i.e., 10 
or more fruit s-1 for each lane). The systems are generally 
expensive and require a large machine vision chamber. 
Therefore they are not suitable for in-field presorting, 
where cost, compactness, and/or mobility are paramount in 
the system design. To reduce the imaging chamber size, 
some machine vision systems have adopted reflective 
mirrors for image acquisition (Cubero et al., 2010). 
However, this design is not desirable or may not even be 
practical in the field because mirrors are susceptible to dust 
and dirt and would need frequent cleaning due to the 
presence of dust and the rugged terrain in the orchard. 
Another option is to use a wide field of view for the 
camera, which, in turn, can cause considerable distortion in 
the acquired images and increase the relative distance 
variation from the camera to the fruit for different locations 
of the image (i.e., the number of pixels per unit dimension 
is not the same over the whole image). Moreover, the 
image processing algorithm, including image acquisition, 
segmentation, features extraction, and classification, must 
be efficient and fast in order to meet the need of sorting 
apples at an appropriate speed (e.g., 4 to 6 apples s-1) with 
limited computer capacity. Furthermore, the machine vision 
system should be energy efficient when it is operating in 
the orchard. These factors, along with other system design 
issues in fruit transport and handling, must be properly 
addressed so that a cost-effective system can be developed 
for in-field sorting and grading of apples. 
Size is one of the most important factors in sorting 
apples into different grades for the fresh market. Many 
methods for fruit size determination have been proposed in 
the past (Moreda et al., 2009). Throop et al. (2003) used the 
major axis obtained from the fitted ellipse for an apple as 
the fruit diameter. Pla et al. (2001) determined the fruit size 
from the maximum and minimum diameters, which were 
calculated based on the moment of inertia of the fruit. 
However, few researchers have tried to estimate the fruit 
size based on the maximum equatorial diameter of the 
equatorial section, which is defined by USDA standards 
(USDA, 2002). This is likely due to the difficulty of 
accurately detecting the stem/calyx end orientation of 
apples. Blasco et al. (2003) determined the equatorial 
diameter of apples using acquired images in which the stem 
was identified to be located nearest to the centroid of the 
fruit. The method worked well when the stem was attached 
to the fruit and was shown in the image. However, when 
apples are rolling on a conveyor, they do not always orient 
themselves in a way that allows the stem to appear in each 
acquired image. Moreover, the stem may be detached from 
some of the harvested apples. To comply with the USDA 
standards for size measurement, it is necessary to detect the 
stem or its location and determine the orientation of each 
fruit in order to estimate the maximum equatorial diameter 
of the equatorial section and differentiate the stem/calyx 
ends from surface blemishes or defects, as the stem/calyx 
ends often exhibit patterns and intensity values that are 
similar to defects, thus resulting in false classifications. 
Previous studies for size estimation have not considered 
the effect of image distortion on fruit size estimation and 
size measurement error caused by the variation of distance 
to the fruit. This is probably because these studies adopted 
a large distance between the camera and the inspected fruit 
and also used sufficient lighting. Image distortion can be 
minimized by using a camera with a narrow field of view 
(or a long focal length), which would limit the imaging 
area, reducing the system throughput rate. Alternatively, the 
system may use a longer distance between the camera and 
objects so that the number of pixels per unit dimension is 
almost the same over the whole image, which, in turn, 
would reduce the image resolution and require more 
lighting or consume more energy because image brightness 
is inversely proportional to the square of the distance. 
These configurations are impractical or undesirable for an 
in-field sorting system that needs to operate within the 
limited spacing between rows of fruit trees in an orchard, as 
well as be mobile and rugged in an outdoor environment 
with a limited supply of energy. 
This article reports on the development of a machine 
vision inspection algorithm to estimate fruit orientation and 
maximum diameter of the equatorial section using a low-cost 
color camera in a close-range view for in-field presorting of 
apples. The specific objectives of the research were to: 
• Assemble a low-cost, compact machine vision system 
suitable for in-field sorting and grading of apples. 
• Develop a simple and fast method to estimate 
variable pixels per unit dimension caused by the 
image distortion and distance variation resulting from 
a close-range view. 
• Determine the orientation of each fruit using moment 
and stem/symmetry detection algorithms. 
• Develop and evaluate an estimation algorithm for the 
maximum equatorial diameter of apples with both 
elongated and round shape characteristics. 
MATERIALS AND METHODS 
MACHINE VISION INSPECTION TEST SYSTEM 
A laboratory machine vision system (fig. 1) was built to 
obtain images and evaluate the accuracy of the proposed 
maximum equatorial diameter estimation algorithm for in-
field sorting of apples. The system consisted of a low-cost 
CCD color camera (Fire-I, Unibrain, Inc., San Ramon, 
Cal.), LED lights, and a low-cost bi-cone roller conveyor. 
The specifications of the machine vision system are shown 
in table 1. The camera was installed 38 cm above the bi-
cone roller conveyor. There were two lanes of bi-cone 
rollers for the conveyor, with 2.54 cm space between the 
lanes. The 2.4 m long conveyor was driven by a hydraulic 
motor at a linear speed of 20 to 30 cm s-1, which would sort 
4 to 6 apples s-1 to accommodate a harvest crew of 6 to

VARIABLE PIXELS PER UNIT DIMENSION ESTIMATION 
Estimating the actual maximum equatorial diameter of 
an object in an image requires determining the number of 
pixels per unit dimension (i.e., mm pixel-1) in the image. 
There were two problems with obtaining the number of 
pixels per unit dimension in the captured images. First, the 
camera had considerable radial distortion because the lens 
had a wide field of view (4.3 mm focal length) to cover the 
required area in the compact machine vision chamber. 
Second, even if radial distortion were zero, the number of 
pixels per unit dimension would not be the same over the 
whole image since the captured image is a perspective view 
(i.e., a projection from the world coordinate to the image 
coordinate system). Image distortion can be corrected using 
the camera calibration method proposed by Zhang (2000). 
After the radial distortion correction, the image was 
transformed to a world coordinate image by the direct 
linear 
transformation 
(DLT) 
method 
(Hartley 
and 
Zisserman, 2004), assuming that the z-coordinate is 
constant and the fruit are flat. Conversion from the raw 
captured image of a calibration checker board to a world 
coordinate image by the DLT method after radial distortion 
correction is shown in figure 3. The calibration checker 
board was placed in the middle of the bi-cone roller 
conveyor where the fruit were being transported. The root 
mean square error of 54 (9 × 6) corner points inside the 
checker board was 0.19 mm when a constant number of 
pixels per unit dimension (i.e., 0.457 mm pixel-1) was 
applied to the whole image. 
Using this world coordinate image and the pixels per 
unit dimension, the accuracy was evaluated for two 
reference balls of 63.5 and 76.2 mm diameter, respectively, 
which cover a typical size range of apples. Images were 
captured for these balls moving on the bi-cone conveyor, 
and the number of pixels and the position of the centroid of 
the balls in the images were measured. The area of the balls 
was then calculated for the raw captured image and world 
coordinate image by the DLT method with radial distortion 
correction. A constant number of pixels per unit dimension 
(i.e., 0.49 mm pixel-1) was used for the whole raw image. 
The area estimation results are shown in figure 4. The 
horizontal axis in the figure shows the pixel location of the 
centroid of the ball along the x-axis, and the vertical axis 
shows the estimated area of the ball using 0.49 mm pixel-1 
for the raw image and 0.457 mm pixel-1 for the corrected 
image. The image segmentation and contour extraction 
methods for measuring the area of the ball are described in 
the next section.

IMAGE PROCESSING PROCEDURE 
The image processing for the maximum equatorial dia-
meter estimation of apples was composed of regions of 
interest detection, segmentation of apples from the 
background, features extraction, apple shape and rotational 
axis 
detection, 
and 
maximum 
equatorial 
diameter 
estimation (fig. 7). Four algorithms, including moment, 
contour extraction, stem detection, and symmetry detection, 
were developed to extract 12 features from the 15 images 
acquired for each apple. Apple shape and rotational axis 
were then calculated from the 12 extracted features of all 
15 images. Finally, maximum equatorial diameter was 
estimated using 12 features of the 15 images, along with 
apple shape and rotational axis information. 
REGIONS OF INTEREST DETECTION 
AND TRACKING OF FRUIT 
Because the images were acquired continuously without 
synchronization, regions of interest for image processing 
were calculated after image acquisition. An example image 
is shown in figure 8. The shafts of the bi-cone rollers were 
painted white to help find the region of interest for image 
processing. The rectangular region, where the shafts were 
located, was converted to a grayscale image. Then the 
intensity profile was obtained by the projection of column 
(or x-axis) intensities in the image onto the horizontal axis, 
as shown in the lower part of figure 8. The intensity profile 
was normalized to between 0 and 1.0 by dividing the 
maximum intensity profile. The shafts positions were 
estimated by detecting the peaks of the intensity profile. 
Peaks were detected using a threshold of 0.3, which was 
decided by the trial-and-error method. Because the shafts 
were lower than the center of the fruit’s transporting plane, 
the position of the shaft locations (peaks) in the image had 
an offset from the position of the fruit plane. This 
perspective error was corrected by a weight function 
proportional to the distance from the center of the image in 
the x-axis, as follows: 
 
pcorrect = pmeasure + w(pmeasure – Iwidth/2) 
(2) 
where pcorrect is the corrected position of the peak, pmeasure is 
the peak position calculated from the intensity profile, w is 
the weight for correction, and Iwidth is the width of the 
image. A value of w = 0.136 was selected manually for this 
research. The number of regions of interest is limited to 
two for each lane. To obtain two regions of interest, three 
continuous peaks were selected. If there were four peaks, as 
shown in figure 8, then either peak p1 or p4 was discarded, 
whichever had a shorter distance to the edge of the image. 
In the case of figure 8, peak p4 was discarded because the 
distance to the edge of the image (e4) was shorter than 
distance e1. The corresponding regions of interest for 
continuous images were tracked by comparing the peaks 
between the continuous images.

Symmetry Detection Algorithm 
The reflectional symmetry of the apple was also 
estimated for more accurate estimation of the maximum 
equatorial diameter in the event that no stem was detected 
for the apple. Many natural objects are reflectionally 
symmetric. Reflectional symmetry has a reflectional line, 
for which the half space is a mirror image of the other half. 
If the reflectional line is detected, it will help to estimate 
the orientation of the apple. Sun (1995) and Sun and Si 
(1999) developed a symmetry detection algorithm using the 
gradient orientation histogram from the grayscale and 
binary images. Li et al. (2006) proposed a fast reflectional 
symmetry detection algorithm using a Hough transform. 
Even though these algorithms were developed to reduce 
computational time, their computational cost for a 
presorting system was still too high (more than 16 ms 
apple-1) based on the preliminary test results. 
In this research, a symmetry detection algorithm using 
the radius function was developed. If the radius function is 
divided into two halves at position x, then the right radius 
function [d(x+k) (k = 1, 2,…, n/2)] covers the right half of 
the radius function from x, and the left radius function [d(x-
k) (k = 1, 2,…, n/2)] covers the left half of the radius 
function from x (fig. 11). Using the right and left radius 
functions, cross-correlation is calculated by the following

maximum equatorial diameter. This requires determining 
the shape of the apple (i.e., elongated or round) and its 
rotational axis (oriented and not oriented). Herein, 
“oriented” means that the rotational axis coincides with the 
stem-calyx end axis of the apple (i.e., less than ±20° from

class is mostly of round shape, but with some fruit in the 
class (e.g., ‘Empire’ and ‘Jonagold’) being elongated in a 
direction perpendicular to the stem/calyx end axis. The 
shape and rotational axis of the apple are estimated based

MAXIMUM EQUATORIAL DIAMETER ESTIMATION 
The maximum equatorial diameter of an apple was 
estimated using the information extracted from the 
15 images. The overall maximum equatorial diameter 
estimation flowchart is shown in figure 13 and explained 
below: 
1. If the number of images with an identified stem is more 
than 10, the maximum equatorial diameter of the apple 
is the average of the maximum equatorial diameters 
calculated from those images with the presence of the 
stem. 
2. Else if the apple is detected as round, the maximum 
equatorial diameter is the average diameter calculated 
for all 15 images based on their areas. 
3. Else if the apple is detected as oriented, the maximum 
equatorial diameter is the average maximum equatorial 
diameter calculated from the symmetry orientation. The 
maximum equatorial diameter is selected as follows:  
3a. If |θMajor – θsym1| < |θMajor – θsym2| for the “long” 
elongation variety (or |θMinor – θsym1| < |θMinor – θsym2| 
for the “wide” elongation variety), the maximum 
equatorial diameter of the apple is the diameter 
perpendicular to the first symmetry orientation (θMajor, 
θMinor, θsym1, and θsym2 are orientations calculated from 
the major moment orientation, minor moment 
orientation, first symmetry orientation, and second 
symmetry orientation, respectively). 
3b. Else the maximum equatorial diameter of the apple is 
the diameter perpendicular to the second symmetry 
orientation. 
4. Otherwise, the maximum equatorial diameter is the 
average 
maximum 
equatorial 
diameter 
of 
the 
combination of the symmetry maximum equatorial 
diameter closer to the major orientation for the “long” 
elongation variety (or minor orientation for the “wide” 
elongation variety) and the maximum equatorial 
diameter calculated from the area. The maximum 
equatorial diameter calculated by the symmetry 
algorithm is used if sdiff of the image is greater than 5%. 
Otherwise, the maximum equatorial diameter calculated 
from the area is used for the image. 
EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURE 
The machine vision system was tested for estimating the 
maximum equatorial diameter of ‘Delicious’ (D), ‘Empire’ 
(EM), ‘Golden Delicious’ (GD), and ‘Jonagold’ (JG) apples 
during the 2009 harvest season. GD and D were predefined 
as the “long” elongation variety, while EM and JG were 
predefined as the “wide” elongation variety in this research. 
One hundred apples each of D, EM, and GD and 70 apples 
of JG were selected for the experiment. These apples were 
in the range of 30 to 90 mm diameter. The apples were 
placed in random orientations on the conveyor running at a 
speed of 30 cm s-1. 
The performance of the orientation estimation methods 
was evaluated by comparing the estimation methods with 
the orientation determined by visual observation. An 
orientation line of the apple in the image was visually 
decided by locating the stem/calyx ends of the apple from 
the image, and this was used as the true orientation.

or reference maximum equatorial diameter of the apples 
was required. Since apples are not perfectly symmetrical or 
round around the stem/calyx axis, it is difficult to 
accurately measure the maximum equatorial diameter 
(e.g., major and minor diameter) of each apple using a 
manual method (e.g., a caliper). Therefore, an in-house 
developed benchtop machine vision system was used to 
measure the true maximum equatorial diameter of these 
apples. The benchtop system consisted of a camera (the 
same as the one installed in the presorting machine vision 
system), a fruit handling device with one end attached to a 
DC motor and the other end adjustable to hold the test 
apple on its stem and calyx ends, and two fluorescent lights

ORIENTATION ESTIMATION 
Table 4 shows the number and percentage of images in 
which the fruit orientation was correctly estimated using 
the stem orientation and symmetry orientation algorithms at 
error levels of ±10° and ±20° when they were compared 
with visual observations for each of the four varieties. The 
success rates for stem orientation estimation within ±20° 
error for D and GD apples were 95.5% and 94.6%, 
respectively. For EM apples, no images were used for stem 
orientation estimation. For JG, only 32 images were 
selected for stem orientation estimation, with a success rate 
of 78.1%. This is because D and GD apples had long and 
straight stems that were easier to detect, while EM and JG 
had shorter and curved stems, which did not appear 
distinctly on the contour. Moreover, D and GD apples were 
more likely to be oriented (i.e., the stem/calyx axis was 
horizontally perpendicular to the moving direction) on the 
bi-cone roller conveyor due to their elongated shape. 
Consequently, these apples were rotating well during the 
imaging, greatly increasing the probability of the stem 
being observed in more than ten images for each apple. 
The symmetry orientation algorithm had the best success 
rate of 77.7% for D apples at the ±20° error level. Most of 
the estimation errors for symmetry orientation occurred for 
apples that had an abnormal elongation direction (i.e., D 
and GD were elongated perpendicular to the stem/calyx end 
axis, and EM and JG were elongated along the stem/calyx 
end orientation). The errors mostly occurred for smaller D 
and GD apples with diameters of less than 63.5 mm. In 
contrast, the rate of incorrect elongation estimations for EM 
and JG was irrelevant to the fruit size. There was no 
consistent pattern for the fruit orientation, even when an 
EM fruit was detected as elongated. The proposed 
orientation estimation method using the moment and 
symmetry detection algorithms was based on the 
assumption that D and GD apples are elongated along the 
stem/calyx end axis, while EM and JG are elongated 
perpendicular to the stem/calyx end axis if they are 
detected as elongated. Poor orientation estimations did not 
greatly affect the result of maximum equatorial diameter 
estimations for EM and JG (see details in the next section) 
since maximum equatorial diameters for most of the EM 
and JG apples were estimated based on the image areas.

CONCLUSION 
A prototype machine vision system consisting of a low-
cost CCD camera, coupled with energy-saving LED lights 
and a two-lane conveyor with generic bi-cone rollers, was 
built as the first step toward achieving the goal of 
presorting apples in the orchard. A simple and fast method 
was developed for accurate estimation of pixels per unit 
dimension from apple images acquired under the close-
range view configuration. Image processing algorithms 
were developed to estimate apple orientation and maximum 
equatorial diameter. Laboratory tests were conducted for 
‘Delicious’ (D), ‘Empire’ (EM), ‘Golden Delicious’ (GD), 
and ‘Jonagold’ (JG) apples at the speed of four fruit per 
second. 
The variable pixels per unit dimension estimation 
method was effective in obtaining accurate spatial 
resolutions based on the fruit’s moving position in the 
images. The orientation estimation algorithm achieved 
87.6% and 86.2% success rates for D and GD apples, 
respectively, at an error level of ±20°, although it 
performed poorly for round-shaped EM and JG apples 
because the orientation of the elongation axis for these 
varieties was inconsistent and could not be estimated 
correctly. The machine vision system was able to estimate 
the fruit maximum equatorial diameter with an overall 
RMSE equal to or less than 1.79 mm for the four varieties 
of apple; it achieved 98% or higher success rate with an 
error of less than ±5 mm. The system had 4.3% 
classification error for undersized apples (i.e., maximum 
equatorial diameter less than 63.5 mm) versus 15.1% for a 
mechanical sizing machine. With the low-cost hardware 
configuration, only 20 ms were needed to process each 
image containing up to four apples. This would be 
sufficient to meet the required presorting speed of 4 to 
6 apples per second and allow implementation of additional 
functions, such as color grading and defect detection, for 
the in-field presorting system that is currently being 
developed.
